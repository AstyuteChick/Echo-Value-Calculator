Energy Regen (ER) – Static and Dynamic values

Thus far, all the sub stats and calculations have assumed the values to be static values. For example, how good Atk% is on Carlotta as compared to crit or Skill% practically does not change based on how much Atk%, crit% or Dmg% Carlotta already has [This is technically not true but is effectively true (or of no consequence) as long as players fit in the assumptions outlined in the assumptions section above].

ER, however, does not work like the other stats we’ve used thus far.

Why even consider ER?

[ER is really important. For some characters like Carlotta, using liberation every rotation is mandatory to do majority of her damage. She is not a character like Brant or the Shorekeeper (who value ER even more), and her ER requirements can be as low as 5% with Zhezhi/Yangyang, or as high as 20 to 25% without them. But regardless, if she does not have this ER, she may waste enough time to lose her buffs before her final liberation hit, which is a major chunk of her damage (on top of the increased rotation time). An Echo that does not recognize ER properly will be severely undervalued (or overvalued, which is just as bad). For characters with optional bursts like Jinhsi, this burst is still a significant portion of her damage even if it is casted every other rotation. For other characters like Camellya, the burst still does a non-negligible portion of Camellya’s damage but more importantly, generates concerto that allows her to do her rotations well].

ER value must be accounted for in both achieved and potential value. Even if we trust users to make sure that they have an appropriate amount of ER for their characters, the issue of determining the value of an Echo is still unresolved. For example, in most cases the users will not exactly have the required amount of ER that is considered safe for a certain character/team/rotation, and even if they do, what numerical value should be assigned to the ER substat on an Echo? There may be certain methods to do this, but the problem itself is too specific and the result diverges away when applied to general cases. It is shown later that even minute differences of 5% more/less ER can quite significantly impact the Echo Scores because 5% is still more than half the average sub stat roll for ER – and if ER is a mandatory ability of the character, than the relative value starts approaching crit.

What is the value of ER?

Since all the previous values were DPS focused, it was easy to adjust them onto the same scale by considering how good they were relative to each other to do their specific job (of increasing DPS). ER affects a character’s DPS only indirectly, and are valuable in other aspects like rotation smoothness and comfort, and hence some further assumptions need to be made to quantify ER on this scale.

[One “scientific” way of determining this (which is not used) would be to consider the amount of unmodified resonance energy generated by the character in question on field, on average or per ability, in a certain time-frame. Combining it with the ER they actually have, it would be possible to convert a point of ER to an equivalent amount of time in seconds. This can then be used to determine the DPS lost in a rotation in an ER deficit situation, which can then be multiplied by the average sub stat value and then converted to the relative value on our scale. This requires lots of character specific data, used in non-specific ways, in a not-so-simple calculation and conversion which has a huge variation range even for a small domain. Furthermore, this method is also subject to many more considerations and improvements if considered seriously. As compared to the alternative method discussed next, this method requires far more effort to increase the accuracy by an insignificant and negligible amount].

ER in achieved value

The [alternate, more practical,] method to evaluate ER would be to assume that the value of an average sub stat roll of ER (9.6%) is dynamic (as opposed to static) and is equal to the value of the highest relative value of the average sub stat rolls of all the stats (which will always be 1) adjusted by how much of that ER is being used/discarded (tentative relative value), and further adjusted with the relative importance of ER.

Dynamic means that the initial tentative relative value of ER used, is changing based on how much ER is already present. A new value, net ER, is defined to be the difference between the total ER a build has minus the recommended ER for the character in the scenario. With this definition, net ER is positive if the build has more ER than necessary. Knowing the net ER allows us to consider if the ER sub stat roll on an Echo is useful, not useful, or precisely how useful.

If net ER is negative, this means all the ER rolls from all the Echoes are being used and are valuable (and this still isn’t enough to reach the target ER). The sub stat roll is divided by ER median roll just like for other sub stats and instead of a relative value multiplier, it is multiplied by a relative importance multiplier (discussed below), thus fully accounting for the ER stat. (The net ER is then passed for further Echo evaluations, if multiple Echoes or a Build is being analyzed. This is discussed later).

If net ER is zero or positive, the sub stat roll is subtracted from net ER. If this new net ER value is (still) positive, all the ER from the echo sub stat is useless and is considered as a dead or non-useful stat, because the amount of excess ER the character has is (still) more than the ER from the Echo’s sub stat. (The new net ER value is passed further if a full Echo build is being processed).

If the prior net ER was positive but becomes negative after subtracting the ER sub stat roll, this means a portion of the sub stat roll was useful, and it is exactly equal to the current negative net ER value (make sure you see why before proceeding). This value is now considered as the actual sub stat roll and then processed like normal as described above (consider the meaning of 0 net ER, and why the process of dealing with positive net ER is the same). (The net ER is then set to 0 because exactly all the ER that is in excess is discarded).

When analyzing a full set of Echoes, the net ER value is updated repeatedly, giving more meaning to the term “dynamic.” For example, if a character has 140 total ER, and their ER requirements are 125, then their net ER is +15. Let’s say they have three Echoes with 10% ER each. For the first Echo, the full 10% ER must be ignored, for the second Echo, only 5% ER from the 10% sub stat roll is considered, and for the third Echo, the full 10% ER is considered. Let’s additionally assume that all these Echoes have the exact same sub stats. For a character who must use their liberation per rotation and it contributes a lot to their role (meaning their relative importance multiplier is high), the difference in the three Echo Scores is very significantly different, despite having the exact same sub stat lines!

Relative importance is a multiplier of ER that scales down the relative value based on the consequences of not using Liberation (made clear with specifics below), and is anywhere between 0 and 1, and it itself is a static value.

Both the dynamic nature and the relative importance multiplier are required because in practical testing, many Echo scores produced completely unjustified and unsatisfactory results when ER was not handled at least this thoroughly, including the handling of ER in the potential value (next section) (again, this is true even for characters where ER is not a big deal like Carlotta when paired with Zhezhi).

It is possible to be even more thorough as mentioned above, but that does not actually produce results that are noticeably and/or practically better/more accurate than the once produced with this method (unless someone using a similar approach figures out a way to handle this, or is willing to brute force all that research and testing and data collection for every character).

Therefore, the relative value of ER is (one multiplied by) the dynamic factor (that considers the current ER surplus/deficit as above), multiplied by the importance factor (that considers DPS loss when the Liberation button is not pressed).

ER in potential value

ER has a huge impact on an Echo’s Potential value as well. Remember that Echo’s potential value is the value of the top 5 most important stats, so depending on the relative values, an Echo’s potential value may change depending upon whether a character’s 5th most useful sub stat has a relative value higher or lower than ER’s current potential relative value. This value is slightly different than the other ER values calculated before. When following how this value is calculated in the next paragraph, remember that this value is supposed to make sure that the Echo potential value is truly the “max” potential an Echo can have.

If a character’s build has negative net ER (meaning they don’t have enough ER), the ER amount on the Echo substat is subtracted from this negative net ER amount (and stored separately as it's only used temporarily to calculate Value A), and is then divided by the ER average sub stat roll, which tells us how many average sub stats away the goal is. If this value (Value A) is less than or equal to -1, the ER’s potential relative value is (1 multiplied by) the character’s ER importance multiplier (because the max value a relative value can take is 1).

If the value (Value A) is greater than -1 (but less than zero since net ER is negative), than this value (Value A) is multiplied by -1, and then multiplied by the ER importance multiplier to get the ER potential relative value. Make sure to understand why this value represents the amount of average sub stat rolls a character is missing, and hence represents the ER’s potential relative value.

If net ER is greater than or equal to zero, the ER sub stat roll is subtracted from this net ER amount. If this value (Value B) is still positive or zero, the ER potential relative value is 0, (and the new net ER value (value B) is passed). This one is easy: ER has no potential for this Echo as the character still has too much excess ER.

If this value (Value B) is negative, this is the amount of ER that is potentially valuable. It is multiplied by -1 and then divided by the average ER sub stat. Now, if this new value (Value C) is greater than 1, the ER potential relative value is (1 multiplied by) the ER importance multiplier, because 1 is the max relative value). The net ER is set to 0, because any more ER sub stat rolls will go through this same process (from the start of this paragraph) and the potential of the Echo will only increase just as much as the ER sub stat roll (which is what we want, since any specific ER for the (next) Echoes is exactly the specific ER that the build requires!).

Else, this value (Value C) must be in between 0 and -1, and it is also multiplied by -1 and then the ER importance multiplier and is now the ER potential relative value, and net ER is set to 0 because all the potential of the ER is accounted for.

Once ER’s potential relative value is calculated, it can then be compared with all the other relative values and the five highest values are added together to finally produce the Echo potential value. The calculated ER potential relative value may or may not be included in the Echo’s potential value, which is another important reason why it must be calculated.

From Echo Score to Build Score

While the build score is simply the average score of the five Echoes in a set, the net ER value is tracked between Echoes as it determines both the achieved score as well as the echo potential score as described above.

It could prove quite useful to re-read the above paragraphs of ER manipulation in both achieved and potential value, not from the perspective of sub stat rolls or achieved/potential value gains, but from the perspective of the changes to net ER. In achieved value section, net ER is there to count every drop of ER that could be deemed as useful, while disregarding everything else. In the potential value section, net ER acts as a control valve: increasing the water flow when the tank is empty, but decreasing it when its full. Having more negative ER than the average ER sub stat roll (empty tank) will boost the value of ER to one multiplied by the importance multiplier. When getting closer and closer to having 0 net ER (i.e. getting closer to the tank’s capacity), this value is reduced closer and closer to 0 until eventually, when the tank is overflowing, all that additional ER sub stat amount (additional water) is disregarded.

Note that regardless of whether an Echo or Build is being analyzed, ER must be treated as above for correct scores in both cases.

[While this above method is useful when users want to analyze all aspects of the build (individual Echoes as well as the overall strength and the weakest links etc.), there is a much easier option for players who only want an analysis of their overall character without going through the cumbersome process of inputting all of their 5 echo sub stats for all their 5 echoes. This is made possible due to Wuthering Wave’s Echo loadout system! On top of being a super convenient feature, highly coveted in Gatcha games, it actually also shows the overall stats a character gets from equipping the loadout. Note that the overall stats a character gets from their Echo sets is simply the Echo main stats plus the Echo sub stats, as it doesn’t consider the set effects or any other benefits (unless I’m mistaken, which jeopardizes this paragraph). Therefore, we can very conveniently find the sum of all the Echo sub stats by subtracting the values of Echo main stats from the displayed amounts, and then treating this as a normal (mega) Echo! The results, not surprisingly, line-up with a full analysis as above. However, note that while in 99% of the cases the scores match literally exactly with the Full Scores or there is a less than 1 point difference, there are cases when there is a discrepancy of more than 5 points (again this is very rare and I’ve personally only observed it doing Cartethyia’s Build analyses].
