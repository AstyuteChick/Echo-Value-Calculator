Achieved Value

Consider an Echo for Phrolova with the following substats (Phrolova allows us to ignore the complexities of ER% for now):

  • 9.3% Crit Rate

  • 15% Crit Damage

  • 8.6% Atk

  • 10.1% Skill

  • 9.4% HP

Other than ER%, the worth of a substat (for a character) is directly tied to how it contributes in increasing the damage the character deals. Importantly, not all the characters place equal worth on the same substats, and not all the substats are as beneficial to a character as each other.

To compare quantities, there must be a common scale. For example, when people calculate CV (Crit Value), they multiply the Crit Rate by 2 and add that to the Crit Damage. The reason behind this is, given a good Crit Ratio, 1% Crit Rate is just as good (or valuable) as 2% Crit Damage, which is why CV is calculated the way it is. In words, this is what’s happening in the calculation: “on a scale where the value provided by 1% Crit Damage is 1, the value provided by 1% Crit Rate is 2. The sum of these two values is called the Crit Value.” (Note that as long as the scales are consistent, we can choose any basis. For e.g. we could have just as easily chosen Crit Rate to have 1 value per 1% CR. But once the scale is fixed, everything else must follow it. On this scale, Crit Damage has 0.5 value per 1% CD. CV calculated this way will just be exactly half of the CV calculated with the prior way, but is still just as accurate and doesn’t lose any function as a metric as long as the metric is kept consistent).

Furthermore, if for example you’re comparing the weather between two places, 32°C is not the same temperature as 32°F (reflecting the importance of consistent scale), and the temperature alone is not the only factor that answers “how good is the weather” (just like how CV does not determine the quality of Echoes on its own). It is known that CV is a very unreliable way of measuring an Echo’s or Build’s strength simply because it doesn’t consider any other factors outside of Crit.

A consistent scale that considers all substats tells us how valuable the substats are relative to each other.

[One way of doing this would be to ask “how much damage increase does 1 additional point of a given substat result in, as compared to other substats”. This approach is not used in the EVC because while it works, there are several problems. For example, during intermediate calculations, it tells us the value of a stat but not a substat. Considering Atk scaling characters, the damage increase by 1 point of Flat Atk relative to 1 point of any other substat is going to be negligible. But note that we could get up to 60 Atk on a substat roll (as compared to the number “9.4” for Atk% or “8.1” for Crit Rate%). For HP scaling characters, the value of 1 point of flat HP will be almost 0 (but you may get 500 in a substat roll), but it will later be shown that Flat Atk or Flat HP substat rolls can, in many situations, be even stronger than some specific attribute% substat rolls (like basic%, heavy%, skill% and liberation%). To be clear, this way could still work, but the values need to be stored to a very high degree of accuracy].

Another way to consider substats’ values relative to each other is to ask, “how much damage increase does an average roll of a given substat result in, as compared to an average roll of another substats.” This gives us a much better idea of the strength of each substat roll. [This also helps a lot in the interpretation of “value” as opposed to “damage increase,” which is especially important when considering ER% or unique/weird characters like Brant later (more on this later). Additionally, it also helps with observing, discovering, and recognizing patterns with specific attribute% substat rolls, which not only helps with calculations, but also makes storing and processing of character data incredibly convenient (for e.g. a single line of code is needed to fully add a new character into the calculator without losing any bit of detail! It consists of 16 values, 15 of which are numbers and many of those are 0! Additionally, the numbers have intuitive meaning (as will be seen shortly) which is a huge advantage when making sure that the character data is accurate and the processing method and resulting scores are correct and sensible)!]

We now ask, “how good is an average roll of a particular substat relative to other stats (for a specific character)?” To find this, we set a “base point” stat level for a character, calculate their damage output in a typical rotation, then compare this to the new damage output of the character when you give them just one of these specific average substat rolls. The difference in the outputs is the damage increase caused by that stat. You then take the stat that results in the highest damage increase (typically crit, or you can take the average of crit rate and crit damage) and then divide every stat’s damage increase by this amount, including the highest one itself. The resulting value for each substat is the relative value of that substat on a scale where the substat that leads to the highest damage increase has the value of 1.

This can be done fully by yourself or using tools like “wutheringtools.com.” I’ve done this for a few characters myself. I’ve also verified the results with the data/tools from TCs like Maygi (she has her own DPS calculator that goes into incredible depth). However, this process is actually not relevant to the EVC, it’s the general observations and the results that are very powerful [just like how you can conclude Gold is rarer or less accessible in nature than Silver by comparing their price, without actually studying economics or understanding how demand-supply works].

The results show some (very interesting and) striking similarities that are shared consistently across all the characters:

On a scale (that we established above) where an average substat roll of Crit Rate or Crit Damage (the substats that result in the highest damage increases) has 1 point of value (in other words, the relative value of crit is 1), the relative value of the stat% a character scales from (Atk%, HP% or Def%) is about 0.5. Furthermore, the flat stat’s relative value (Flat Atk, Flat Def and Flat HP) is almost always half of that, so around 0.25. Furthermore, a very clear pattern is observed for the relative value of a specific attribute substat rolls (basic%, heavy%, skill% and liberation%) across all the characters – [since Atk% and Element% are very close in effectiveness for almost all characters with a working build (a fact that surprisingly many are unaware of), and since Element% and Attribute% are additive (for our context, it means they’re the same thing), I theorized that their relative value should be the same as the relative value of Element% (~Atk%) times the ratio of this type of damage a character deals in the rotation]. For example, if a character deals 85% of their damage as skill damage, then the relative value of Skill% should be \(0.5*0.85 = 0.425\). And sure enough, this is the case every time for all characters when calculating damage increases per average substat rolls manually [Note that when this is not the case (for weird characters like Brant), we can just as simply use the manually calculated version. Also note that the values only need to be roughly the same, as all the deviations observed cause practically zero change to the Echo Scores (a fact you can also easily verify yourself later!]!

[An important mathematical fact about substats that many will find interesting is as follows: The sum of the relative value of all the attribute bonuses (basic, heavy, skill, liberation) is always less than or equal to 0.5. This is simply because the sum of the overall damage contribution from these attributes is always less than or equal to 100% (as a matter of course). Since the (relative) value of a fully general attribute bonus is the same (on average) as Atk%, the relative value of an average roll of Atk% sub stat will always be greater than or equal to all the relative values of the average roll of attribute dmg% sub stats combined. Practically, this means that if you have an Echo that gives (average rolls of) Basic%, Heavy%, Skill% and Liberation%, this Echo will always be weaker than an Echo that just gives an average roll of Atk%, no matter what character or situation].

Example Calculation

Let’s calculate the achieved value of the above-mentioned Echo for Phrolova:

  • 9.3% Crit Rate

  • 15% Crit Damage

  • 8.6% Atk

  • 10.1% Skill

  • 9.4% HP

First, we count the number of average substat rolls for each substat (\(\frac{SV}{SA}\)):

  • Crit Rate: \(\frac{9.3}{8.4} = 1.107\)

  • Crit Damage: \(\frac{15}{16.8} = 0.893\)

  • Atk%: \(\frac{8.6}{9} = 0.956\)

  • Skill%: \(\frac{10.1}{9} = 1.122\)

  • HP%: \(\frac{9.4}{9} = 1.044\) (this is an unnecessary step of course, as Phrolova doesn’t scale from HP)

Next, these numbers are multiplied by their respective relative values of the average substat rolls to produce the relative values of these particular substat rolls (it may be useful to consider that the \(no.\ of\ avg*\ \frac{value}{avg} = value\ of\ avg\)) (\(*RV\)):

  • Crit Rate: \(1.107*1 = 1.107\)

  • Crit Damage: \(0.893*1 = 0.893\)

  • Atk%: \(0.956*0.5 = 0.478\)

  • Skill%: \(1.122*0.225 = 0.252\)

  • HP%: \(1.044*0 = 0\)

The sum of these values is (\(\sum_{i}^{}{(\frac{{SV}_{i}}{{SA}_{i}}*{RV}_{i})}\)): \(1.107 + 0.893 + 0.478 + 0.252 = \mathbf{2.73}\), which is the achieved value of this Echo for Phrolova.
