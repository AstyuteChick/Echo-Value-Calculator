Potential Value

[After finishing the achieved value section, keen readers who’re also familiar with other Gatcha-game gear systems (Genshin in particular) may have noticed some similarities with the achieved value calculations and what’s commonly called as “counting substats” when determining the worth of Artifacts, for example. You may think of Artifacts as an Echo with 8 to 9 stat lines, where you may roll previously rolled stats but there must be at least 4 unique stats.

To determine the worth of an Artifact, you simply count how many average rolls of each (useful) substats you have. Counting, in total, 5 to 6 or more per Artifact is considered a good piece []. This method is very quick, very easy, and very practically accurate too. It’s so easy, in fact, that no calculator (specialized or general) is required and simply glancing at the Artifacts for a few seconds is all it takes to determine its worth.

The major and key differences between this and the calculations in the achieved value section are caused due to the difference in balancing approaches of substats in the gearing systems between Wuthering Waves and Genshin Impact. In GI, the average substat roll of crit (3%/3.3% cr or 6%/6.6% cd, iirc) approximately provides the same value as an average roll of Atk% (5%), and for stats like flat Atk, average effective values from average Atk% substats rolls are considered (so every 35ish flat Atk counts as 1 roll). All of these average substat amounts increase the damage of a character by roughly 2.2% iirc.

However, this method of looking at all the stats, dividing them by the average value, and counting how many you have (in other words, exactly the first step of the calculations in the achieved value section) only works by itself if the stats provide roughly the same damage increases for a character (and across all characters of the same scaling-type). And the reason why “5/6 or more” counted useful substats is a good thing is because there is an inherent yet hidden assumption that, at most, the Artifact can only go up to 9 on average (meaning high-rolls are balanced by low-rolls and most rolls are close to average). In Wuthering Waves, however, there is no clear upper point that applies to all characters and these upper points are very significantly different to each other, and moreover, this upper point can also significantly change from one Echo to the next for a specific character, as will be shown later! Of course, there are many caveats that could be discussed (for both games), but they don’t affect the essence of this discussion].

We calculated the achieved value of 2.73 for Phrolova with a given Echo. However, this value on its own does not tell us the worth of the Echo. Just like points on an exam, 50 points means two completely different things if the potential number of points achievable in an exam are 50, or 100.

Therefore, to calculate this potential value of an Echo for Phrolova, we consider an Echo that’s the hypothetically best Echo Phrolova could have. This means an Echo that has 5 substats that Phrolova values the most, all rolled at max. The 5 most valuable substats for Phrolova are the 5 substats with the greatest relative values. For Phrolova, they are Crit Rate, Crit Damage, Atk%, Flat Atk and Skill%.

Rather than considering an Echo that has these substats rolled at maximum, it is very important to consider an Echo that has all these substat rolled exactly at average instead. One of the main reasons for doing this is because considering max rolled Echoes provides no intuitive insight about the Echo’s score (highly important in the section discussing analysis of Echoes and Tier Brackets), and it’s simply “less fun” (rather than numbers without meaning, this approach makes the Echo scores have practical meaning). This point will be elaborated in detail later, and will be much obvious with examples later on.

Finally, here is our “hypothetically best Echo” for Phrolova, with the best 5 substats rolled exactly at average:

  • Crit Rate: 8.4%

  • Crit Damage: 16.8%

  • Atk%: 9%

  • Flat Atk: 45

  • Skill%: 9%

The achieved value of this hypothetical Echo (“hypothetical” because it is not possible to roll these substats) is defined as the Potential Value of an Echo for Phrolova. It is easy to see that the potential value is equal to (\(\sum_{i = 1}^{5}{ORV}_{i}\)): \(1 + 1 + 0.5 + 0.25 + 0.225 = \ 2.975\).
