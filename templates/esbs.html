Echo Scores and Build Scores

As we now have the achieved value and potential value of an Echo, we can now calculate the Echo Score, \(ES = \frac{AV}{PV}*100\) . Here is the Echo again that we considered for Phrolova:

  • 9.3% Crit Rate

  • 15% Crit Damage

  • 8.6% Atk

  • 10.1% Skill

  • 9.4% HP

We calculated its achieved value to be 2.73, and its potential value to be 2.975. Therefore, this Echo’s Score is \(\frac{2.73}{2.975} = \mathbf{0.918\ or\ 91.8}\)! (This Echo is quite insane! Even though the score is multiplied by 100 and is technically a percent, the % sign is omitted to not cause confusion when scores exceed 100). We can verify this on the EVC website: 91.774 – Extreme tier!

Build score is simply the average of all 5 Echo scores in a set, but as we will see, it’s only really so straightforward for Phrolova alone. Practically speaking, ER has a huge impact on the quality of an Echo for characters that care about having ER, even if the ER amount they require is very low (even one ER roll requirement (around 10%) can significantly impact the score of an Echo, and can have a substantial effect of the overall build score (as it should!). This will be justified and elaborated later).

Consequences and interpretations of Echo Score

Potential Value of an Echo is unique to the particular Echo

[There is a subtle yet deliberate choosing of words when discussing the potential value of an Echo. Although the potential value of an Echo does not (yet) seem to rely on the specifics of a particular Echo itself, the wording used is always indicating that the calculated potential value of an Echo belongs to this particular Echo alone, and may be different for another Echo for the same character. This is true for most characters, and cases like Phrolova are the exceptions, which is why she was chosen to simplify the discussion of prior topics].

Echo Score reflects the quality of an Echo

[Consider the following Echo:

  • Crit Rate: 7.5%

  • Crit Damage: 16.2%

  • Atk: 7.9%

The remaining two are dead stats. Let’s calculate this Echo’s Score for Ciaccona and Carlotta. We start with the achieved value:

\[AV = \frac{7.5}{8.4}*1 + \frac{16.2}{16.8}*1 + \frac{7.9}{9}*0.5 = 2.296\]

This achieved value applies to both Ciaccona and Carlotta. Now let’s look at the potential value for this Echo for both Ciaccona and Carlotta (we assume both of them have sufficient ER to ignore the complexities of ER for now). The relative value tables for both of them are as follows:

Carlotta:

Substat Name: Crit Rate Crit Damage Atk% Flat Atk HP% Flat HP
Substat Relative Value: 1 1 0.5 0.25 0 0
Def% Flat Def Basic% Heavy% Skill% Lib% ER%
0 0 0 0 0.425 (=0.5*0.85) 0 0-0.6

Ciaccona:

Substat Name: Crit Rate Crit Damage Atk% Flat Atk HP% Flat HP
Substat Relative Value: 1 1 0.5 0.25 0 0
Def% Flat Def Basic% Heavy% Skill% Lib% ER%
0 0 0.1 (=0.5*0.2) 0.075 (=0.5*0.15) 0.025 (=0.5*0.05) 0.125 (=0.5*0.25) 0-1

Considering both character’s best stats, their potential value for this Echo is:

\[{PV}_{Ciaccona} = 1 + 1 + 0.5 + 0.25 + 0.125 = 2.875\]

\[PV_{Carlotta} = 1 + 1 + 0.5 + 0.425 + 0.25 = 3.175\]

Subsequently, this same Echo gets a score of \(ES_{Ciaccona} = 79.9\) and \(ES_{Carlotta} = 72.3\) despite having the same achieved value for both the characters.

It’s important to reflect on why this is the case.

This shows that Echoes cannot be judged on their own and their environment (characters and their stats) determines their worth.

The reason why this Echo gets a higher score on Ciaccona is because the potential best value Ciaccona can have on an Echo is lower than the value Carlotta can get from an Echo, because Carlotta’s damage is concentrated in one damage type – namely Skill%. Ciaconna’s damage is spread out between multiple types. Remember that, compared to Atk% substat roll, specific attribute substat rolls can only buff a part of a character’s damage profile which, of course, is equal to the ratio of this type of damage the character does to the general attribute damage%. So, by nature, the relative values of \(Basic\% + Heavy\% + Skill\% + Liberation\% < Atk\%\) (technically speaking the inequality holds for General Attribute Damage% rather than Atk%, and in this context, that’s the same as saying the inequality holds for Element% (because Element% and General Attribute Damage% are additive), and since on a fully built and geared character, Element% is roughly equal to Atk%, it gives us the logical explanation of why the given inequality is observed in character’s relative values). Or in other words, \(Basic\% + Heavy\% + Skill\% + Liberation\% < \mathbf{0.5}\). Since an Echo can only have 5 lines, the more concentrated your damage type is the more value you can potentially squeeze on an Echo, which lowers your Echo Score, but it also means that your Echo substats matter more for the actual amount of damage your character is doing comparatively.

This shows yet another advantage of using this method. If the score focused on damage increases specifically rather than an Echo’s quality and value to the characters, the craziest and strongest Echoes you may roll on characters like Ciaccona would still get a lower score than obviously less rare and less crazy and less exciting Echoes on Carlotta (and this effect is even more pronounced when factoring in ER). The scores obtained like this become meaningless and would require, again, to set different goals for different characters, indirectly requiring the addition of the potential value system anyways!]

Same Echo Scores on different characters

[The meaning of this situation should be apparent by now. If Ciaccona and Carlotta have two different Echoes but the Echo Score they get are exactly the same, respectively, then in terms of actual damage, Carlotta’s Echo is increasing Carlotta’s damage more than Ciaccona’s Echo is increasing Ciaccona’s damage.

If two people score 75% on a test, but one took a 100-point test while the other took a 200-point test, it’s obvious that in terms of actual points, the second person scored 150 points which is much higher than the 75 points scored by the first person. Of course, this still means that both are equally good at the subject (just like how the Echo’s quality is the same for each character respectively, they’re equally impressive) but one just has more actual points earned (like Carlotta’s Echo leading to higher DPS gain comparatively). And of course, the difference isn’t this pronounced and is highly exaggerated in this example, and it’s more like the difference between taking a 100-point test and a 120-point test].
